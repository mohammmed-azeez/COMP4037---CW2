About the Dataset

This analysis draws on NHS England Hospital Episode Statistics (HES) — the most comprehensive record of inpatient hospital care in England. The dataset covers 26 financial years from 1998–99 through 2023–24, recording every admitted patient episode across all NHS trusts. Each year's data is broken down by ICD-10 primary diagnosis, capturing finished admission episodes, emergency admissions, waiting list admissions, mean length of stay, and mean patient age. The 2018–19 financial year is used throughout as the pre-pandemic baseline. Data was obtained directly from the NHS England digital statistics portal and processed from 26 separate annual Excel releases.

⚠ Post-Pandemic Backlog Alert

Categories showing red in 2022 and 2023 represent a structural healthcare crisis — not a temporary disruption. These conditions had fewer patients treated after COVID while simultaneously accumulating longer waiting lists. The most severely affected include circulatory disorders, HIV disease, and certain injury categories, none of which have returned to pre-pandemic admission levels by 2023-24.

How to Read This Chart

The 2018-19 column is your zero reference — all other years show percentage change from that baseline. Dark red in 2020-21 shows the COVID lockdown impact. Categories still red in 2023-24 are the ones that never recovered. Hover over any cell to see the exact percentage change for that category and year.

Key Insight

The Backlog Quadrant — Where Are the Gaps?

The vertical dashed line at x=0 is the admissions baseline — left of it means fewer patients treated. The horizontal line at y=0 is the waiting list baseline — above it means more people waiting. Conditions where the mean patient age clusters above 60 and recovery remains negative represent a compounding demographic risk — older populations who often have fewer alternative options for care and longer recovery times if treatment is delayed. Backlog-trapped categories skewing older deserve policy prioritisation.

Conclusion

What the Data Tells Us

By 2023–24, most NHS diagnosis categories had broadly returned to or exceeded their pre-pandemic admission volumes. The COVID-19 crisis caused a sharp universal drop in 2020–21, but for the majority of conditions the healthcare system adapted and throughput recovered within two to three years. However, the aggregate picture conceals a structurally important minority.

A subset of diagnosis categories remains in what this analysis terms the backlog trap — conditions where admission volumes are still below 2018–19 levels while waiting lists have simultaneously grown beyond pre-pandemic size. This combination is not a temporary disruption; it represents a sustained mismatch between demand and delivery that worsened through the recovery period rather than resolving. The most affected categories include circulatory system disorders, HIV disease, certain infectious conditions, and injury-related categories — none of which returned to baseline by 2023–24.

The multi-variable approach taken here — combining a recovery heatmap, parallel coordinates analysis, hierarchical treemap, patient profile radar chart, and correlation scatter matrix — reveals dimensions of this crisis that no single chart could expose. The backlog is not uniform across disease types, not uniform across patient age groups, and not uniform across emergency versus elective pathways. Targeted policy intervention requires exactly this level of granularity.

Still Trapped

Multiple high-burden categories remain below 2018–19 admission levels in 2023–24 despite four years of recovery time, with waiting lists that have grown substantially above baseline.

Compounding Risk

Backlog-trapped categories skewing toward older patient populations carry compounded risk — delayed treatment for older patients typically leads to longer hospital stays and worse outcomes when care eventually arrives.

Policy Implication

Standard throughput metrics mask these structural traps. Policymakers need simultaneous visibility of admission rates, waiting list trends, and patient demographics to identify which conditions require targeted intervention rather than general capacity increases.
